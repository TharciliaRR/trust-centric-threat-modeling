# Abstract

For decades, cybersecurity has focused on protecting information systems through confidentiality, integrity, and availability controls.

As autonomous AI systems become increasingly responsible for decision-making, organizations face a new challenge that traditional security frameworks were not designed to address:

Trust.

While security helps protect systems, trust determines whether organizations are willing to rely on autonomous decisions.

This research note proposes the concept of **Trust Engineering** as an emerging discipline focused on designing, measuring, monitoring, and continuously improving trust in autonomous AI systems.

The central hypothesis is that trust will become a measurable organizational capability, similar to security, resilience, and governance.

---

# Introduction

The history of technology can be understood through the evolution of confidence.

Organizations adopted computers because they trusted their calculations.

They adopted networks because they trusted connectivity.

They adopted cloud computing because they trusted infrastructure providers.

Today, organizations are beginning to adopt autonomous AI systems because they trust their ability to assist or perform decisions.

However, unlike traditional software, autonomous systems actively influence outcomes.

They recommend actions.

They prioritize options.

They generate conclusions.

They increasingly participate in decisions previously reserved for humans.

This creates a new challenge:

> How do we engineer trust in systems that make decisions?

---

# The Limits of Traditional Security

Traditional cybersecurity frameworks focus on protecting assets.

Examples include:

* Confidentiality
* Integrity
* Availability
* Authentication
* Authorization

These controls remain essential.

However, they do not fully answer questions such as:

* Can the decision be trusted?
* Can the recommendation be explained?
* Can confidence be measured?
* Can trust degradation be detected?
* Can decision integrity be monitored?

An autonomous system may be technically secure while still producing outcomes that stakeholders do not trust.

This observation suggests that trust deserves its own engineering discipline.

---

# Defining Trust Engineering

## Proposed Definition

Trust Engineering is the discipline of designing, measuring, governing, and improving trustworthiness in socio-technical systems.

The objective of Trust Engineering is to ensure that stakeholders can maintain justified confidence in the behavior, decisions, and governance of autonomous systems.

---

## Core Principles

Trust Engineering should be based on five principles:

### Measurability

Trust must be measurable.

Organizations cannot govern what they cannot measure.

---

### Transparency

Trust requires visibility into system behavior.

Opaque systems reduce confidence.

---

### Accountability

Trust requires identifiable ownership and responsibility.

---

### Reliability

Systems must consistently behave according to expectations.

---

### Resilience

Trust must survive failures and adversarial conditions.

---

# Trust as a System Property

Traditional engineering disciplines focus on specific properties.

| Discipline              | Primary Property |
| ----------------------- | ---------------- |
| Software Engineering    | Functionality    |
| Security Engineering    | Protection       |
| Reliability Engineering | Availability     |
| Safety Engineering      | Harm Prevention  |
| Trust Engineering       | Confidence       |

This perspective positions trust as an explicit design objective rather than an indirect outcome.

---

# Components of Trust Engineering

## Trust Measurement

Organizations require mechanisms to quantify trust.

Potential metrics include:

* Decision Accuracy
* Explainability Scores
* Accountability Coverage
* Governance Compliance
* Stakeholder Confidence

---

## Trust Monitoring

Trust should be continuously monitored.

Examples:

* Trust Dashboards
* Decision Quality Monitoring
* Confidence Tracking
* Behavioral Drift Detection

---

## Trust Assurance

Organizations require assurance mechanisms.

Examples:

* Independent Reviews
* Validation Processes
* Trust Audits
* AI Assurance Programs

---

## Trust Governance

Trust requires oversight.

Examples:

* Trust Policies
* Governance Committees
* Accountability Frameworks
* Executive Reporting

---

# Trust Engineering and Autonomous AI

Autonomous systems increase the importance of trust.

Human users no longer evaluate every action.

Instead, they rely on:

* Recommendations
* Prioritization
* Autonomous reasoning
* Automated execution

This delegation of authority creates a dependency on trust.

The greater the autonomy, the greater the need for trust engineering.

# Conclusion

The rapid adoption of autonomous AI systems introduces challenges that extend beyond traditional cybersecurity.

Organizations must now consider how to establish and preserve confidence in decisions produced by intelligent systems.

This research note proposes Trust Engineering as a new discipline dedicated to addressing that challenge.

Just as Security Engineering emerged to protect digital assets, Trust Engineering may emerge to protect confidence in autonomous decision-making.

The future of AI will not be determined solely by intelligence.

It will be determined by trust.

---

# Research Proposition

> Trust should not be treated as a byproduct of secure systems. Trust should be engineered, measured, monitored, and governed as a first-class organizational capability.
