# 3. Limitations of Traditional Threat Modeling

## 3.1 Introduction

Threat Modeling has long been recognized as one of the most effective approaches for identifying, assessing, and mitigating security risks throughout the system development lifecycle.

For decades, organizations have relied on established methodologies to understand attack surfaces, prioritize risks, and implement appropriate security controls. Frameworks such as STRIDE, PASTA, Attack Trees, and more recently MITRE ATLAS and the OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications have significantly improved the ability of security teams to anticipate and address emerging threats.

However, the rapid adoption of Autonomous AI Agents introduces a category of risks that extends beyond the original design assumptions of these frameworks.

Traditional threat modeling approaches were primarily developed to evaluate systems that:  

- execute deterministic logic;
- operate within predefined boundaries;
- follow explicitly programmed workflows;
- produce predictable outputs.
  
Autonomous AI agents challenge these assumptions.

They reason probabilistically, continuously adapt to changing contexts, interact with external systems, and increasingly participate in business decision-making processes.

As a result, organizations must address not only technical vulnerabilities but also risks affecting trust, governance, accountability, and decision integrity.

These dimensions remain insufficiently represented in existing threat modeling methodologies.

## 3.2 STRIDE: Strengths and Limitations

Developed by Microsoft, STRIDE remains one of the most influential threat modeling methodologies in cybersecurity.

The framework classifies threats into six categories:  

| Category | Objective |
|-----------|-----------|
| Spoofing | Identity Protection |
| Tampering | Integrity Protection |
| Repudiation | Accountability |
| Information Disclosure | Confidentiality |
| Denial of Service | Availability |
| Elevation of Privilege | Authorization |

STRIDE provides an effective mechanism for identifying vulnerabilities within software architectures and system interactions.

Its structured approach allows organizations to systematically evaluate attack vectors and security weaknesses.

### Strengths

STRIDE excels at identifying:

- unauthorized access;
- data manipulation;
- privilege escalation;
- service disruption;
- identity-related attacks.
  
These remain critical concerns in AI environments.

### Limitations in Autonomous Systems

Despite its effectiveness, STRIDE was not designed to evaluate the quality or trustworthiness of decisions generated by autonomous agents.  
For example:

An AI agent may remain fully compliant with STRIDE controls while simultaneously producing:

- biased recommendations;
- strategically misaligned actions;
- inconsistent decisions;
- unreliable outputs.
  
None of these conditions necessarily represent violations of confidentiality, integrity, or availability.

Yet they can produce substantial organizational harm.

STRIDE evaluates whether systems are secure.

It does not evaluate whether systems remain trustworthy.

## 3.3 PASTA: A Risk-Oriented Approach

The Process for Attack Simulation and Threat Analysis (PASTA) introduced a more business-oriented perspective to threat modeling.

PASTA focuses on:

- business impact;
- attacker motivation;
- attack simulation;
- risk assessment.
  
This represents a significant advancement compared to purely technical methodologies.

### Strengths

PASTA acknowledges that security must be evaluated within business contexts.

The framework supports:

- risk prioritization;
- threat simulation;
- business alignment.
  
These capabilities remain highly valuable for enterprise AI deployments.

### Limitations in Agentic AI

PASTA assumes that risks emerge primarily from malicious actions targeting systems.

However, autonomous agents introduce an additional challenge:

Organizations may experience harm even in the absence of malicious attacks.

Examples include:

- decision drift;
- hallucinated recommendations;
- unintended actions;
- objective misalignment;
- excessive autonomy.
  
These failures often emerge from the interaction between autonomy, context, and decision-making rather than from conventional attack scenarios.

PASTA does not provide explicit mechanisms to evaluate decision integrity or trustworthiness.

## 3.4 MITRE ATLAS: Advancing AI Threat Modeling

MITRE ATLAS represents one of the most important developments in AI security.

The framework catalogs adversarial techniques targeting machine learning and AI systems.

Its objectives include:

- understanding attacker behavior;
- identifying AI attack techniques;
- improving defensive capabilities.
  
MITRE ATLAS provides a valuable foundation for organizations deploying AI technologies.

### Strengths

ATLAS addresses threats such as:

- model poisoning;
- adversarial examples;
- model theft;
- model evasion;
- training data attacks.
  
It significantly expands the cybersecurity community's understanding of AI-specific threats.

### Limitations in Enterprise Agentic Systems

MITRE ATLAS primarily focuses on adversarial attacks against AI systems.

While essential, this perspective does not fully address risks associated with:

- governance failures;
- accountability gaps;
- trust degradation;
- operational autonomy;
- decision quality.
- 
An autonomous agent may operate without experiencing any ATLAS-defined attack while still making harmful or strategically misaligned decisions.

Consequently, organizations require additional mechanisms for evaluating trust-related risks.

## 3.5 OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications

The OWASP Top 10 for LLM Applications emerged in response to the rapid adoption of generative AI technologies.

The framework identifies critical risks including:

- Prompt Injection;
- Sensitive Information Disclosure;
- Supply Chain Vulnerabilities;
- Excessive Agency;
- Overreliance on LLM Outputs.
  
This represents a major step forward in securing AI-enabled applications.

### Strengths

OWASP successfully highlights several threats highly relevant to autonomous agents.  
Notably:

**Prompt Injection**  
Demonstrates how decision processes can be manipulated through crafted inputs.  
**Excessive Agency**  
Highlights risks associated with excessive autonomy.  
**Sensitive Data Disclosure**  
Addresses privacy and information security concerns.  

These categories align closely with emerging enterprise challenges.

### Limitations

Despite its relevance, OWASP remains primarily focused on security vulnerabilities.

The framework does not explicitly evaluate:

- trustworthiness;
- decision consistency;
- governance maturity;
- organizational confidence;
- long-term operational reliability.
  
Organizations require mechanisms that extend beyond identifying vulnerabilities and begin evaluating trust as a measurable security objective.

## 3.6 The Missing Dimension: Decision Integrity

A common characteristic across existing frameworks is their focus on protecting systems.

Autonomous agents introduce a new requirement:

Protecting decisions.

Decision Integrity can be defined as:

 _The ability of an autonomous system to consistently produce outcomes that remain accurate, aligned, explainable, auditable, and supportive of organizational objectives._
        
This concept introduces several dimensions absent from traditional threat models:

**Alignment**  
Are decisions aligned with organizational goals?  
**Consistency**  
Are similar situations producing similar outcomes?  
**Explainability**  
Can decisions be understood and justified?  
**Predictability**  
Can stakeholders anticipate behavior?  
**Accountability**  
Can responsibility be established?  

These factors directly influence organizational trust.

## 3.7 The Trust Gap

The limitations identified across existing frameworks reveal what this paper defines as the Trust Gap.

**Traditional Security Question**
Is the system secure?

**Emerging AI Question**
Can we trust the system's decisions?

While related, these questions are fundamentally different.

An autonomous agent may satisfy traditional security requirements while simultaneously exhibiting:

- decision drift;
- inconsistent behavior;
- governance deficiencies;
- opaque reasoning;
- loss of stakeholder confidence.
  
Current frameworks provide limited visibility into these conditions.

As organizations increase reliance on autonomous systems, this gap becomes increasingly significant.

## 3.8 Toward Trust-Centric Threat Modeling

The emergence of agentic AI requires an evolution in threat modeling practices.

Organizations must continue evaluating:

- confidentiality;
- integrity;
-availability;
- adversarial attacks.
- 
However, they must also assess:

- trust;
- governance;
- accountability;
- operational resilience;
- decision integrity.
  
These dimensions collectively determine whether autonomous systems can be safely integrated into critical business processes.

The Trust-Centric Threat Modeling Framework (TCTM) is proposed as a response to this challenge.

Rather than replacing existing methodologies, TCTM extends them by introducing a structured mechanism for evaluating the trustworthiness of autonomous AI systems.

In the following chapter, we introduce the conceptual foundations of the TCTM framework and its role in addressing the Trust Gap in enterprise autonomous agents.

## Key Takeaway
Existing threat modeling frameworks remain essential for securing AI systems, but they were not designed to evaluate the trustworthiness of autonomous decision-making. As organizations increasingly depend on agentic AI, security must evolve from protecting systems alone to protecting the integrity, governance, and trustworthiness of the decisions those systems produce. This evolution forms the foundation of the Trust-Centric Threat Modeling Framework (TCTM).
