# 7. Trust Risk Matrix

## 7.1 Introduction

Risk assessment has always been a cornerstone of cybersecurity.

Organizations routinely evaluate threats based on two fundamental dimensions:
- Impact
- Likelihood
  
Traditional risk models generally assume that if these dimensions are adequately measured, organizations can effectively prioritize security investments and mitigation efforts.

For conventional information systems, this approach has proven highly effective.

However, autonomous AI agents introduce a new challenge.

Many of the most significant risks associated with autonomous systems are not fully captured by operational or financial impact alone.

A decision generated by an AI agent may:

- be technically incorrect;
- undermine stakeholder confidence;
- violate governance expectations;
- erode trust in organizational processes.
  
The resulting damage may exceed the direct operational consequences of the incident itself.

This observation leads to a fundamental premise of the TCTM framework:
 ```text
Trust is both an asset and a risk factor.
```
The Trust Risk Matrix (TRM) was developed to incorporate this dimension into enterprise risk assessment.

## 7.2 Why Traditional Risk Models Are Insufficient

Most cybersecurity frameworks evaluate risk using a variation of the following equation:

 ```text
  Risk = Impact × Likelihood
  ```
This model works well when evaluating risks such as:

- data breaches;
- ransomware attacks;
- denial of service incidents;
- unauthorized access.

However, autonomous systems create additional concerns.

Consider the following scenarios:

### Scenario A
A customer support agent incorrectly answers a customer inquiry.
**Operational impact:**
Low.
**Financial impact:**
Low.
**Trust impact:**
Moderate.

### Scenario B
A financial AI agent produces an incorrect investment recommendation used by executives.
**Operational impact:**
Moderate.
**Financial impact:**
High.
**Trust impact:**
Critical.

### Scenario C
An autonomous compliance agent repeatedly generates inconsistent regulatory recommendations.
**Operational impact:**
Moderate.
**Financial impact:**
Moderate.
**Trust impact:**
Critical.

In each example, trust becomes a material risk factor.

Traditional models fail to explicitly account for this dimension.

## 7.3 Introducing Trust 

The TCTM framework introduces a third risk variable:

### Trust Impact
**Definition**
The degree to which a threat affects confidence in the reliability, integrity, governance, or decision-making capabilities of an autonomous system.
Trust Impact measures organizational confidence degradation rather than purely technical consequences.

### Core Principle
Two incidents with identical operational impact may produce dramatically different trust outcomes.
**For example:**

| Incident | Operational Impact | Trust Impact |
|----------|-------------------|--------------|
| Temporary API Failure | Moderate | Low |
| Hallucinated Executive Recommendation | Moderate | Critical |

This distinction becomes increasingly important as organizations rely on autonomous systems for strategic decisions.

## 7.4 The TCTM Risk Formula
The Trust Risk Matrix expands traditional risk calculations.
### Traditional Model
Risk = Impact × Likelihood

### TCTM Model
Risk = Impact × Likelihood × Trust Impact
Where:
**Impact**
Measures organizational consequences.
**Likelihood**
Measures probability of occurrence.
**Trust Impact**
Measures degradation of organizational confidence.

## 7.5 Risk Scoring Methodology

Each dimension is scored using a five-point scale.

## Impact Scale

| Score | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Negligible |
| 2 | Minor |
| 3 | Moderate |
| 4 | Significant |
| 5 | Severe |


## Likelihood Scale

| Score | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Rare |
| 2 | Unlikely |
| 3 | Possible |
| 4 | Likely |
| 5 | Almost Certain |


## Trust Impact Scale

| Score | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| 1 | Minimal Trust Degradation |
| 2 | Limited Confidence Reduction |
| 3 | Noticeable Trust Impact |
| 4 | Significant Trust Erosion |
| 5 | Critical Loss of Confidence |


## 7.6 Trust Impact Assessment Criteria
To improve consistency, organizations should evaluate Trust Impact using five dimensions.

### Decision Reliability
Can stakeholders continue relying on outputs?

### Governance Confidence
Can leaders demonstrate appropriate oversight?

### Regulatory Confidence
Can decisions withstand regulatory scrutiny?

### Stakeholder Confidence
Do users continue trusting the system?

### Organizational Reputation
Could confidence loss damage reputation?

Each dimension may be independently assessed and aggregated into an overall Trust Impact score.

Move beyond purely technical impact.

##  7.13 Toward Trust-Aware Risk Management

The evolution of enterprise AI requires a corresponding evolution in risk management.

Traditional approaches focus on protecting systems.

Autonomous systems require organizations to protect confidence in decisions.

The Trust Risk Matrix represents a practical mechanism for achieving this objective.

By introducing Trust Impact as a measurable variable, organizations gain the ability to identify, prioritize, and mitigate risks that would otherwise remain invisible within conventional cybersecurity assessments.

## 7.14 Chapter Conclusion
The Trust Risk Matrix expands traditional cybersecurity risk assessment by introducing Trust Impact as a third dimension of risk.

This innovation allows organizations to evaluate not only the operational consequences of an incident but also its effect on confidence in autonomous systems.

As enterprises increasingly rely on AI agents to support critical decisions, the ability to measure trust-related risk becomes essential.

The Trust Risk Matrix provides the quantitative foundation required to transform trust from an abstract concept into a governable security objective.

## Key Takeaway
In autonomous AI systems, the most damaging incident is not always the one that causes the greatest technical disruption. It may be the one that destroys confidence in the system's ability to make trustworthy decisions. The Trust Risk Matrix makes that risk visible.
